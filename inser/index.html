Insertion Sort

25 Jan 2016

There are many types of sorting algorithms, which vary by time complexity, space complexity and efficiency. The insertion sort method is quite popular, easy to implement, and is how most people manually sort cards.

Insertion sort splits the array into sorted and unsorted. You will have to iterate through the array to move the beginning of the sorted section and then iterate through that sorted section to find the correct spot for the current element.

var array = [3,8,2,6,1];
[3, <-sorted | unsorted-> 8,2,6,1]
[3,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 2,6,1]
The first element to sort will be at array[1], the first element of the unsorted section of the array. We take the value at array[1] and compare it to all elements of the sorted array. If the element is smaller, then the two numbers are swapped.

[3,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 2,6,1]
[3,8,2, <-sorted | unsorted-> 6,1]
[3,2,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 6,1]
[2,3,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 6,1]
Now the first element of the unsorted array, array[2], will be compared with the elements in the unsorted array and placed in it's proper position. 2 is compared with 8 and swapped, then 2 is compared with 3 and swapped.

[2,3,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 6,1]
[2,3,8,6, <-sorted | unsorted-> 1]
[2,3,6,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 1]
This pattern continues until all elements have been sorted.

[2,3,6,8, <-sorted | unsorted-> 1]
[2,3,6,8,1 <-sorted | unsorted-> ]
[2,3,6,1,8 <-sorted | unsorted-> ]
[2,3,1,6,8 <-sorted | unsorted-> ]
[2,1,3,6,8 <-sorted | unsorted-> ]
[1,2,3,6,8 <-sorted | unsorted-> ]

And we are left with our final result.
[1,2,3,6,8]

Pros: simple implementation, efficient for small data sets, constant space complexity as it sorts in place

Cons: impractical for sorting large arrays because its average time complexity is quadratic
